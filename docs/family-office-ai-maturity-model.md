# Family Office AI Maturity Model

A practical model for where a family office or RIA sits on the path to using AI well — and what the next step is. It is descriptive, not aspirational: most offices are at Level 0 or 1, and that is a reasonable place to be. The point of the model is not to rush to Level 4, but to advance deliberately, with governance keeping pace with capability.

Higher is not automatically better. A Level 1 office with good confidentiality discipline is in a stronger position than a Level 3 office that scaled workflows faster than its review habits.

---

## The five levels at a glance

| Level | Name | One-line state |
|-------|------|----------------|
| 0 | No formal AI use | AI is not used, or its use is unknown |
| 1 | Individual experimentation | Staff use AI ad hoc, without rules |
| 2 | Standardized workflows | A few workflows are defined and reused |
| 3 | Governed operating system | Workflows run under governance and review |
| 4 | AI-native family office | AI is a governed operating layer across functions |

---

## Level 0 — No formal AI use

**Description.** The office does not use AI for its work, or any use is incidental and unmanaged. Work is done manually.

**Common behaviors.** Manual briefing, manual diligence write-ups, no AI policy, no shared practice.

**Risks.** Mostly opportunity cost — and "shadow" use creeping in without rules (staff quietly using consumer tools on sensitive material).

**Governance posture.** None specific to AI.

**Example workflows.** All manual.

**Next step.** Decide deliberately whether and where to pilot AI. Before any pilot, set the confidentiality and governance guardrails (see [ai-governance-for-family-offices.md](ai-governance-for-family-offices.md) and [privacy-and-confidentiality.md](privacy-and-confidentiality.md)).

## Level 1 — Individual experimentation

**Description.** Individuals use AI on their own initiative, in varying tools, without shared rules or review standards. Useful, but uneven and risky.

**Common behaviors.** A staff member drafts a brief or summarizes a document with whatever tool is handy; practices differ person to person; no record of what is used.

**Risks.** Confidential data in unapproved tools; inconsistent quality; over-reliance on unverified output; no audit trail. This is the level where most confidentiality incidents happen.

**Governance posture.** Minimal or informal.

**Example workflows.** Ad hoc document summaries, ad hoc drafting.

**Next step.** Establish the basics: an approved environment with acceptable data terms, the confidentiality rules, and a simple human-review expectation. Then standardize one workflow (move toward Level 2).

## Level 2 — Standardized workflows

**Description.** The office has defined a small number of repeatable workflows (e.g., the weekly brief, document digests) used consistently, with a shared format and a review habit.

**Common behaviors.** A pilot workflow is run the same way each time; outputs use a consistent template; a person reviews before use.

**Risks.** Standardization can breed complacency — treating a familiar output as trusted; review discipline slipping under time pressure; governance lagging the number of workflows.

**Governance posture.** Emerging: an approved tool, a review expectation, basic confidentiality rules.

**Example workflows.** [Principal Weekly Brief](../skills/principal-weekly-brief/), [Document Digest](../skills/document-digest/), [Post-Meeting Action Extractor](../skills/post-meeting-action-extractor/).

**Next step.** Wrap the workflows in governance: written approved/prohibited use cases, review tiers by consequence, recordkeeping, and training (move toward Level 3). See the [Implementation Guide](implementation-guide.md).

## Level 3 — Governed operating system

**Description.** AI workflows run inside a defined governance framework. Use cases are written down, review is tiered by consequence, recordkeeping exists, and staff are trained. The office can answer "what do we use AI for, and how is it reviewed?"

**Common behaviors.** Multiple workflows across functions; named accountability; a maintained approved-use list; measurement of activity and review completion.

**Risks.** Scaling workflows faster than governance; metrics that flatter (overstated time/value); integration temptation that could erode the human-in-the-loop model.

**Governance posture.** Defined and maintained: governance document, privacy rules, review tiers, recordkeeping, training, periodic review.

**Example workflows.** The full skill set used across communication, operations, diligence, and decision support — each under review.

**Next step.** Extend deliberately across the operating-model layers while holding the human-review and confidentiality lines (move toward Level 4). See the [Operating Model](operating-model.md) and [Measurement Framework](measurement-framework.md).

## Level 4 — AI-native family office

**Description.** AI is a governed operating layer across the office's functions — intake, research, communication, operations, analytics, deal flow, institutional memory, and decision support — with humans retaining every consequential judgment. Capability and governance advance together.

**Common behaviors.** Consistent, measured workflows across functions; strong institutional memory; principals receive well-prepared, well-framed material; governance and review are habitual, not occasional.

**Risks.** Over-confidence and over-reliance at scale; automation creep removing humans from the loop; governance treated as "done" rather than maintained. The failure mode at this level is subtle: the office trusts the system more than it should.

**Governance posture.** Mature and actively maintained, with the explicit principle that AI absorbs structure-heavy work while humans keep judgment, relationships, and risk. Guardrails are understood as instructions, not enforceable controls.

**Example workflows.** The operating model in [operating-model.md](operating-model.md), realized across layers — still read-only, still human-in-the-loop.

**Next step.** Sustain it. Re-examine governance on a regular cadence; resist autonomy creep; keep human review and confidentiality discipline as the load increases.

---

## Using the model

- **Locate yourself honestly.** Most offices are at 0–1. That is fine.
- **Advance one level at a time.** Skipping governance to chase capability is how offices get into trouble.
- **Let governance lead, not trail.** The right time to write the rules is before you scale, not after an incident.
- **Remember the ceiling.** Even a Level 4 "AI-native family office" is read-only and human-in-the-loop. AI never holds judgment, authority, or professional responsibility.
